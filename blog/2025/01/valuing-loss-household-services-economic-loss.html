1. Defining Household Services

Household services encompass activities such as:

  • Meal preparation and cleanup
  • Interior cleaning and laundry
  • Home maintenance and repairs
  • Childcare and eldercare tasks
  • Household management (e.g., billâ€‘paying, shopping)

These tasks are typically performed by the claimant or family members, free of charge, yet they free other household members to work and participate in society. Failure to replace these services imposes economic and intangible burdens.

2. Valuation Approaches

Two principal methods prevail for valuing lost household services:

2.1 Replacementâ€‘Cost Approach

Under this approach, analysts estimate the cost of hiring a qualified providerâ€”for example, a housekeeper, nanny, or homeâ€‘health aideâ€”to perform the same tasks. Replacementâ€‘cost rates are derived from market data on wages plus an overhead markup for employer costs (e.g., taxes, benefits).

2.2 Opportunityâ€‘Cost Approach

This method values household hours at the claimant's own afterâ€‘tax wage rate, on the theory that time spent on domestic tasks precludes market employment. It is used when claimants could otherwise earn wages by reducing household time.

Courts, however, typically favor the replacementâ€‘cost approach for its objectivity and direct reflection of market rates (National Association of Forensic Economics, 2021).

3. Data Sources for Replacement Costs

3.1 American Time Use Survey (ATUS)

The ATUS provides annual estimates of time spent on household tasks by U.S. residents (Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2025). In 2024, individuals averaged 2.01 hours per day on household activitiesâ€”14.07 hours per week (Bureau of Labor Statistics).

3.2 Occupational Employment and Wage Statistics (OEWS)

The OEWS program reports mean wages for domestic workers. As of May 2023, maids and housekeeping cleaners earned an average of $16.66 per hour nationally (Bureau of Labor Statistics). 3.3 Consumer Expenditure Survey (CES)

The CES tracks average household spending on services, including childcare and housekeeping. While less granular, it can corroborate wageâ€‘based rates (U.S. Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2025).

4.1 Determining the Lost Period

Identify the period during which the claimant was unable to perform domestic tasksâ€”e.g., 6 months of recuperation.

4.2 Calculating Average Weekly Hours

Use ATUS data or the claimant's timeâ€‘use diary to determine average weekly hours lost. For a fullâ€‘time homemaker, 14.07 hours per week is a reasonable baseline (Bureau of Labor Statistics, 2025) (Bureau of Labor Statistics).

4.3 Total Lost Hours

$$\text{Total Hours Lost} = \text{Weeks Lost} \times \text{Hours per Week}$$

For 26 weeks at 14.07 hours/week:

$$26 \times 14.07 \approx 366 \text{ hours}$$

In cases of permanent disability, estimate the claimant's remaining life expectancy and assume a decline in hours over time (e.g., due to aging). Use Social Security Administration life tables for survival probabilities and adjust hours by a participation factor (Saurman & Means, 1989).

$$\mathrm{PV} = C \times H \times \frac{1}{(1 + r)^t}$$
